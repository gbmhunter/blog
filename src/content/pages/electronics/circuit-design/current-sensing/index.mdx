## Low-Side Resistive Current Sensing

One of the most basic ways to measure current is to use a low-side current sensing resistor. Benefits of this method are:

- Simple and cheap
- Single-ended output due to resistor being on the low-side. This means you can feed a single signal into an ADC (because it is referenced to GND).

<Aside type="tip">
High-side current-sense resistors require differential measurements.
</Aside>

The below image shows the oscilloscope voltage on the top-side of a low-side 100mΩ resistor connected to a small brushed DC motor as it is turned on. The voltage peaks at 280mV, which corresponds to 2.8A ($I = \frac{V}{R}$). It then drops and settles down to approx. 90mV, which corresponds to 0.9A. I think the noise is due to the switching action of the motor's brushes.

The following image shows a schematic for a low-side current sensing circuit that can be connected to an ADC. This can be used for applications such as brushed/BLDC motor phase current measurement (see the [BLDC Motor Control page](/electronics/circuit-design/bldc-motor-control/)). It uses a current sense resistor on the low side of the current path (i.e., next to ground) and then level-shifts the result to $ \frac{V_{cc}}{2} $ via the $R_2$/$R_3$ divider so that current flowing in *both* directions can be measured (e.g. the bidirectional phase current in a motor drive). The trade-off is that this halves the gain and raises the source impedance seen by the ADC. $R_2$, $R_3$ and $C_1$ also form a low-pass anti-aliasing filter — from $C_1$'s perspective the two resistors appear in parallel (since the supply is an AC ground), giving a cutoff of $f_c = \frac{1}{2\pi R C} \approx 31.8\unit{kHz}$ (where $R = R_2 \parallel R_3 = 5\unit{k\Omega}$).

<Image src={import('./_assets/low-side-basic-current-sensing-circuit.webp')} width="650px">A schematic for a low-side current sensing circuit that can be connected to a microcontroller/MCU ADC.</Image>

The value of the current-sense resistance is a trade-off between resolution and power dissipation/circuit disruption. The higher the current-sense resistance, the larger the voltage signal for a given current and hence better resolution. However, a larger voltage means both more power dissipation in the resistance AND greater disruption to the circuit you are trying to measure. Assuming a resistive load, disruption means the load begins to see a reduced voltage and draws less current (the very current you are trying to measure!).

Generally speaking, choosing a resistance that drops a few hundred millivolts at max. current is a good starting point. This voltage can be fed directly into a MCU ADC if you just want a rough current measurement, or can be passed through an [op-amp amplifier stage](/electronics/components/op-amps/) first to increase the voltage to match the full-scale range of the ADC.

Another thing to consider is whether or not you need Kelvin sensing. If you just take a single tap of the top-side of the resistor to your ADC, you are measuring the voltage drop across the resistor AND the ground plane between the resistor and the ADC. This drop across the ground plane could introduce a significant error in your measurement if the resistance of the current sense resistor is in the same order of magnitude as the ground plane resistance. To compensate for this you can perform Kelvin sensing (four-terminal sensing) by taking separate feeds back to the ADC from both the top and bottom of the resistor, however this will require a differential measurement. You can buy dedicated [resistors](/electronics/components/resistors/) with four-terminals for this exact use case.
